# Reviewer's Comments:

This study investigates the dependence of photoevaporation rates on disk size by performing a suite of hydrodynamic simulations using the temperature prescription of Picogna et al. (2019). The authors found that while the surface mass-loss rate is independent of disk size, the cumulative mass-loss rate scales with disk size. They also report that applying this updated prescription leads to longer disk lifetimes of compact disks whose sizes are comparable to the critical radius for photoevaporation.
The manuscript is organized; however, some parts are hard to understand, and there are several critical issues that must be addressed before the results can be considered reliable. Furthermore, I see no compelling reason for this paper to be published as a Letter. The study does not present an urgent or particularly impactful finding, and addressing the key issues identified below will likely require additional analysis and discussion that exceeds the limitations of the Letter format. 
Without resolving these concerns, I cannot recommend the paper for publication.

1.	Overestimated mass-loss rates: 
Sellek et al. (2024) demonstrated that the mass-loss rates obtained with the Picogna et al. (2019) prescription are overestimated by roughly an order of magnitude due to underestimated cooling rates. Since the present study adopts the same computational framework, the resulting mass-loss rates are likely overestimated by a similar factor. 
Indeed, the reported rate of ~1e-8 Msun/yr at 20 au (Fig.2) is already about ten times higher than typical photoevaporation rates found in Sellek et al. (2024) and earlier radiation hydrodynamic studies (e.g., Wang & Goodman 2017; Nakatani+18a,b; Komaki+21), which assumed disk sizes of ~100au.
Furthermore, recent long-term disk evolution studies indicate that adopting photoevaporation rates of ~1e-8 Msun/yr produces disk population properties inconsistent with observations, whereas rates around ~1e-9 Msun/yr are more consistent (e.g., Sellek+20; Weder+23; Lienert+24,25; Tabone+25). 
Therefore, the mass-loss rates presented here appear significantly overestimated and may be physically unrealistic, even for compact disks with r_1 ~ 10au. Publishing such results without proper justification risks misleading the field. Unless the authors can clearly justify the continued use of the classical approach adopted by Picogna et al. (2019), I cannot recommend this paper for publication. 

We thank the referee for raising this important point.
Absolute X-ray–driven photoevaporation rates remain uncertain at the factor-few to order-of-magnitude level because they are sensitive to the microphysics of heating and cooling and to the incident spectrum. Recent PLUTO+PRIZMO calculations that include additional cooling (notably O–H collisional excitation) report integrated wind rates <= 10^-9 Msun yr-¹ for solar-type stars—about an order of magnitude below earlier X-ray models that neglected these channels (Sellek et al. 2024). These results are consistent with earlier thermochemistry-consistent RHD studies that tended toward lower rates and emphasised sensitivity to coolant inventories and dust treatment (e.g. Wang & Goodman 2017; Nakatani et al. 2018). Moreover, composition alters not only the normalisation but also the radial distribution of the mass loss: carbon/metal depletion (a proxy for sub-solar metallicity in the wind-launching layers) modifies the cooling balance and thus the surface mass-loss profile itself (Wölfer et al. 2019). In view of these uncertainties, our scientific focus here is on the dependence of the wind on disc outer radius, i.e. how compact discs rescale otherwise similar local profiles, rather than on fixing a unique absolute normalisation.
To facilitate comparison with recent work while keeping the radius-scaling analysis transparent, we recomputed all models with a uniform Mdot(Picogna prescription) / 10 renormalisation of the mass-loss rates, mirroring current practice in the literature. We stress that a single global factor is a practical interim choice, if chemistry/metallicity varies across systems (or even within a single disc), both the normalisation and the radial profile can change (e.g. Wölfer et al. 2019; Sellek et al. 2024). Our work therefore report results using the scaled values, and readers may rescale the normalisation as future constraints evolve. 
We have now added a discussion of these points in the Methods section (Section 2.3), clarifying the physical motivation for adopting the scaled prescription and explicitly noting that the absolute rates should be regarded as uncertain by at least a factor of a few. The relative trends with disc size and the resulting implications for inside-out dispersal and population statistics — which are the main focus of our work — remain robust against such a global renormalization.

Therefore, applying a radius-scaling relation derived from simulations for a 1Msun star to systems with different stellar masses is not physically valid, and I expect the normalization factor should also depend on stellar mass.

4.	Limited physical insight: 
We thank the referee for this comment. We agree that, at a qualitative level, the outer disc exerts little dynamical influence on the photoevaporative flow launched near the gravitational radius, and this behaviour is indeed expected. However, this invariance of the surface mass-loss rate with respect to disc size is not presented as a central result of our work. Rather, it is a necessary premise that allows us to define and test a quantitative scaling of the integrated mass-loss rate with outer radius in the compact-disc regime.
As described in Section 3.1 and shown in Figure 2, the radiation–hydrodynamic simulations performed with PLUTO were designed to confirm that the cumulative mass-loss rate can be accurately recovered by integrating the nearly invariant surface profiles up to the disc’s critical radius, r1. They were initially intended to test whether, near the critical radius, photoevaporation results in a genuine mass loss or whether the gas is instead recirculated back into the disc at slightly larger radii, as found in similar contexts by Weber et al. We verified that this difference is minimal, confirming that the effective reach of photoevaporation lies close to, but slightly within, the critical radius. This check validates our approach of truncating the cumulative mass-loss integration at r1 and justifies the use of the derived scaling for compact discs.
Once this behaviour was established, we incorporated the resulting scaling into the viscous-evolution and population-synthesis models, which constitute the main focus of the paper. It is in this broader context that the quantitative scaling provides new physical insight: it modifies disc dispersal timescales, naturally produces inside-out clearing, and improves agreement with observed accretion-rate distributions and disc fractions.
In summary, while we agree that the near-invariance of the surface mass-loss profiles is qualitatively expected, our study provides, for the first time, a quantitative scaling of the total mass-loss rate verified through radiation–hydrodynamic simulations and applied self-consistently to compact discs in population-synthesis calculations. The PLUTO simulations were a necessary step to confirm the physical validity of this scaling and to test the detailed flow behaviour near the critical radius, thereby providing the foundation for the evolutionary analysis that represents the principal advance of this work.
